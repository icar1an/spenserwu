# on skincare

I've already been asked by n>1 people to un-gatekeep everything I've learned about skincare and help them with product recommendations. This is the best way to democratize all my knowledge, because gatekeeping things are for losers.

Let's start with the basics. The first and most important thing about skincare is understanding your skin type. You can spend all the money on products, get fancy treatments, do everything you could possibly do with money, and still completely mess up because you don't understand your skin type. There are a few different ones:

- Oily
- Normal
- Combination
- Rosacea
- Acne-prone

There are also hybrids — people who have dry patches in some areas, normal skin elsewhere, and oily skin in others. Really try to spend a lot of time understanding your skin. There are fantastic resources on the internet, but anecdotally I can say that I know for sure I have very oily skin, because by the end of the day I look like somebody rubbed a layer of argan oil all over my face. That's just the way my skin is, so I get products that are best for oily skin.

A few things about products. There are going to be people who say, "Oh, get all your stuff from Korea," or "Get all your stuff from La Roche-Posay," or whatever binary rule. I would say definitely ignore all of that. Naturally, some skincare brands are going to have more chemistry when you mix products from the same line, because they were meant to be used together. But when you're first starting out, you don't really know what works for you, so you should spend a lot of time exploring.

This is essentially the multi-armed bandit problem from operations research. When you don't know what works best for you, you should spend a lot of time exploring before you settle down and commit to a few options. That's just the most rational thing to do.

One more thing on products: avoid fragrances in your skincare. You want them to be minimally scented, and if they are scented, make sure it's the natural scent and not anything crazy, because that will irritate your skin. You do not need scented skincare products. Remember this.

Once you've figured out your skin type, here's another really good rule: less is more. Don't go buy 15 different serums and three different creams — it's completely unnecessary. Try to keep your routine as minimal as possible, because there's always new stuff coming out and there's always probably going to be a product out there that works better for you at any given point in time.

If you commit really heavily to tons of different ingredients and they mix together in certain ratios, it can lead to adverse side effects. For example, if you have a serum with 10% niacinamide but your cream also has 5 or 6% niacinamide — even though individually those are fine on their own, when you mix them together you're going to have an adverse reaction. You'll think it was one product when in reality it was just mixing two things that were incompatible.

The rule here: keep the amount of actives in your routine as minimal as possible. Please do not be doing an AHA/BHA acid peel with very aggressive exfoliation and then also doing adapalene. You're going to completely destroy your skin barrier.

A warning on actives like adapalene and any form of retinol — retinol, retinoid, they all accomplish the same thing. They're vitamin A derivatives that increase cell turnover on your face, and in the beginning they have purge phases. Honestly, if you think you need adapalene, just start with adapalene. Don't go crazy. You don't need to go to a dermatologist. If you do go see one, they probably know more about this than I do. I just refuse to see a dermatologist because I wanted to figure this all out myself, and I think I did.

Adapalene is great. It's the strongest topical retinoid you can get over the counter at 0.1% concentration, and it works magic. In the beginning there will be purge phases. Be prepared to slather your face with ceramide creams multiple times a day. I did it during the winter of my freshman year here at Cornell and it was brutal. I literally carried a tub of CeraVe cream with me and would be slathering it on my face because it literally felt like it was on fire. You stick through that, and your face is going to be baby smooth by the end of it. My face used to feel all rocky and pocketed; now it's baby smooth, all because of adapalene.

Another massive win for my skin was ceramides. There are, I think, three different types of ceramides that can nourish your skin barrier. They're great because they're just fatty lipids that go directly into your skin barrier and absorb well. If your skin's oily, you might think, "Wait, why would I put more ceramides on my face? Shouldn't my face have enough?" Well, if you do put ceramides on your face, your skin will be moisturized and will therefore produce less oil, because producing oil is a reaction to your skin being a little dry.

The first time I got into ceramides was through CeraVe cream, but that's way too heavy because they put mineral oil in it. You could use it during the winter, but during the summer it's absolute overkill — your face will feel greasy and heavy. It worked during the winter but only at night, and when I wake up in the morning I want something on my face that isn't that heavy. So I looked at the active ingredients, realized that NP ceramide was the most important one, and that's how I came across Pyunkang Yul. It's the skincare brand I obsessively recommend to essentially anyone looking for a simple stack that solves the vast majority of their problems. They do a line for normal skin, combination skin, acne-prone skin — everything's great, minimal ingredients. It came from a Korean hospital that just ended up making their own brand. They don't do any advertisements whatsoever, and it's been completely fantastic.

A huge win for my oily skin was double cleansing. You start by taking a cleansing oil — I used to use the Muji one, then I switched to Manyo, and now I'm using the FANCL cleansing oil. That's the one from Japan with minimal preservatives; you have to use it up within 90 days. It's probably the absolute best one you can buy with your money.

The point of the oil cleanse is to dissolve all the junk in your pores — your sebum, basically. You rub the oil into your face in upward, swirling motions. Just look up a K-beauty tutorial on how to do this, and then let it sit on your face for 10–15 minutes.

Then you get in the shower. Take some warm water and emulsify the oil, so now you have this white film on your face. Leave that on while you're showering and doing other things. Then scrub it off once that's done. Follow up with a gentle cleanser — I really recommend the 1025 Dokdo cleanser from Round Lab. It's nourishing, great in general, and doesn't over-strip the moisture from your face. You can use the birch one, but I really think the Dokdo is the single best cleanser from that line. I also recommend this green mung bean cleanser — I completely forgot the brand, it comes in a green tube, something like "B.Young" — it's also great and smells fantastic.

After you double cleanse, you can hit your usual skincare routine — toner, serum (whatever serum ended up working for you), cream — and you're basically set.

A few other random pointers:

Sometimes it's nice to use an exfoliating cleanser. I've had good results with glycolic acid, which is an AHA (alpha hydroxy acid). You could also do BHA, which is salicylic acid. I like the COSRX one — quite great for smoothing out your skin.

Clay masks are great, especially if you have oily skin. Try to do those at least once a week after your shower. They're perfect for getting rid of oil, sebum, and junk on your face, and some form of physical exfoliation is just great for your skin in general. Keeps it fresh.

Trick for sheet masks: put some heavy ceramide cream on your face, then take a hydrating sheet mask and slap it right over the cream.

For hydration, think about something like hyaluronic acid, but be very careful — if you apply it to dry skin, it will suck all the moisture out of your face. Make sure your face is damp before you apply it. That's how you lock in the moisture and get your face looking real smooth.
