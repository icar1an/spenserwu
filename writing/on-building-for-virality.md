# on building for virality

This project originally started as some form of exploration into advertising technology, but that's awfully boring, and I ended up getting very distracted by Instagram Reels during this time. It was in my apartment — or one that I'm generously living out of. Thank you so much, Ashley, for making this possible.

Essentially, I realized that people always try to build a great product first and then do great marketing second. What if we completely flipped that thesis on its head and built something purely for marketing, with product second? If you just invert the order, what comes out? You naturally start thinking: what would look the absolute dumbest on Instagram Reels?

I had this random, fleeting thought about a guy named Aaron Kahn who built an Arc'teryx detector. It just uses MediaPipe to detect if you're wearing Arc'teryx on your head, and if so, it pulls up a quiz that asks you to name ten mountains. That's kind of funny, and it's exactly the energy I was thinking about when building for virality first. Honestly, a complete waste of MediaPipe's potential anyway.

The immediate idea that came to mind was: what if somebody was playing the Chrome Dino game with their head? You have to move your head up to make the dinosaur jump every time, and it would look so, so stupid. Ben didn't think this was possible. I thought he was completely wrong, so I vibed out a very basic working version in half an hour. And naturally, if you can do the Chrome Dino game, what's the Chrome Dino game but with three other cardinal directions? Subway Surfers. You literally end up building this in like two hours.

Once we had a working product, we thought it'd be hilarious to just take it to West Campus and do guerrilla marketing. Just find random people in the libraries and dorms and ask them to try playing with it. The reception was insane, because I don't think anybody has ever seen someone play Subway Surfers with their head. I ended up skipping class the next day to rapidly improve head mode: adding automatic tracking, perfecting sensitivity thresholds for gestures and hand-based commands. The way you perfect the technology is hilariously by watching a ton of short-form content and playing Subway Surfers. And that's when it hit me: if it works for games, it works for scrolling. You could literally scroll Instagram Reels with your head, and it's kind of perfect for that.

Ben got the thing into an auto-updating DMG, sped through the website, and we put it all together in like 30 minutes. Cue a bunch of UI/UX improvements.

When you use a product a lot, you end up having this great intuition for what to build next. The entire time, I never once felt like I was the one actually in charge of the product, if that makes sense. The product literally made itself, and that's the most fun thing about building for a consumer when you are the consumer. You just know what should exist next.

The one interesting thing about building silly products is they're basically infinite content engines. You can do so much random stuff with them. You just go to lectures and scroll brainrot. There have probably been many people at Cornell who've seen me do this. I don't want my permanent legacy to be the guy who scrolls Instagram Reels with his head in lectures. I do way more than that.

A disgusting side use case of something like this is scrolling multiple Reels at the same time, so you can pull up multiple devices. If I added iris tracking, you could just flick between them. Though I will say, doing this will definitely hurt your head, so I do not recommend trying this at home.

I will say, fundamentally this is a MediaPipe wrapper. It's not a serious project — at this time, I hadn't fully conceived of the idea of Unserious Ventures. I fully intended to take this a lot further, but after doing it I realized it stopped feeling as fun, and that's the irony of trying to take the equivalent of a shitpost idea seriously. There's zero, zero point in doing something like that.
